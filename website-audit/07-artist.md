## Main Text

Jess Linton is a visual artist and creative practitioner who works independently and within collaborative projects to explore the world around her. She has worked within the area of social upheaval and political conflict for many years and over the past five years, domestic abuse. She is interested in exploring a range of art mediums but also found objects and other materials, for sparking curiosity and greater understanding about the themes arising from this work. Often her artwork develops in partnership with an element of social engagement or social action practice.

Minutes after the first devastating earthquake in Nepal (2015), Jess witnessed children turning to play, seeking refuge in their own creative spaces; rebuilding 'home' – a place to dwell, be nurtured and supported. Jess observed displaced communities in Calais (Art Refuge UK, 2015–ongoing) drawing on the arts as an embedded act of survival; sharing cultural traditions or connecting through contemporary digital media; reclaiming some sense of freedom, voice, and identity; attempting to make sense amidst turbulent times. In working with people who have experienced physical, or emotional, displacement, finding a place to belong; a home "not only the place but the cluster of feelings [and identities] that are associated with it" (Papadopulos, 2002) is a recurring theme in her artwork.

Jess regularly works with local and natural materials, and in 2019 she founded The Plot — a creative outdoor wellbeing space in Stanmer Park, Sussex, for sparking shared creativity, community spirit and connection. Her recent inquiry involves digging for and working with local clay whether a tiny palm held object or a larger scale community oven. In her work in Nepal during the devastating earthquakes (2015), natural materials became grounding tools during difficult conversations, and symbols of how small and temporary, yet astonishing and special life could be. Working with natural materials and the natural world offered rich discussion around important themes for Nepal at the time: preservation and history; holding on; letting go; acceptance; rites of passage; cycles of life. In Sussex, an area built on clay, a common ground is shared which might hold the answers to breaking down borders and opening up compassionate conversation in a challenging climate. In 2021 and 2022, Jess co-facilitated clay community builds.

Inspired by posters of protest and disheartened by mainstream media, Jess is an avid documenter of her work and daily life, whether through photography or the collections and re/assemblage of borrowed images; articles; old magazines; reference books; postcards from women in Scarborough she has never even met.
